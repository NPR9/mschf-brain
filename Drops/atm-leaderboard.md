# ATM Leaderboard

**Archive #:** 140
**Year:** 2022
**Format:** Modified ATM / interactive installation
**Source:** *Made by MSCHF* Book p.296–300+ (Absurd Objects section) + Transcripts (HIGH)
**Confidence:** HIGH

## One-Line Concept

A fully functional ATM at Art Basel Miami Beach that photographs you and displays your bank balance on a public leaderboard — ranked against everyone else who used it that day. The richer you are, the more it makes fun of you.

---

## Full Description

ATM Leaderboard is a stock ATM machine modified with one upgrade: when you insert your card, it photographs you, reads your checking account balance, and displays both your face and balance on a digital leaderboard screen mounted above the machine.

The leaderboard ranks all users by available funds in real time. Custom animations trigger based on rank:
- **High balances:** Bowling alley celebration screen; Abraham Lincoln pulling off his face to reveal Jeff Bezos; scenes from luxury life
- **Low balances:** "SAD," "POOR," derogatory custom animations
- **Top 10:** "RICH" designation, special treatment

**Exhibited:** Art Basel Miami Beach 2022, in Perrotin's gallery booth. Sold as sculpture for $75K.

## The Concept in One Line

> "Conceptually, the higher you are on the leaderboard, the more the work is making fun of you."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.298

This inverts every status display dynamic at Art Basel. The fair is structured around conspicuous wealth signaling. ATM Leaderboard makes that signaling literal, public, and humiliating at the top.

## What It Is (Description Quote)

> "ATM Leaderboard is a literal visualization of the subtext present at any art fair: the conspicuous display of wealth. The work consists of a fully functional ATM with an upgrade: when you insert your card, the device photographs you and records your checking account balance, then displays your picture and balance on a digital leaderboard screen. The leaderboard ranks users according to their available funds at the time of use."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.298

> "ATM Leaderboard is a combination of object, interaction, and context. Conspicuous consumption reaches its apex at Art Basel Miami Beach, among a dense concentration of Lamborghini rentals, Rolexes, and contemporary art purchases. The fair forms the perfect backdrop for the work, which initially infiltrates the space as a functional object (a mundane ATM), and secondly as an artwork in the Perrotin art gallery booth."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.298

## Genesis: 3-Year Wait (Test of Time in Action)

> "MSCHF's first internal records of ATM Leaderboard date to 2019, and from the outset, the piece was conceived as an installation for Art Basel Miami Beach, which we identified as the best (perhaps only) place to install the work. At the time, we did not know how to place a piece into an art fair. We optimistically cold-called the administrative phone number on Art Basel's website and asked, 'Hey, can we put a piece in here?' to no avail."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.299

**The timeline:**
- 2019: Idea conceived. Cold-called Art Basel. Rejected.
- 2019–2022: Idea held in "idea pool" — not abandoned, waiting for conditions to be right
- 2022: Gallery representation via Perrotin enables Art Basel access. Drop executed exactly as originally conceived.

This is the **Test of Time principle at 3-year scale** — the idea survived not months but years without production, emerged unchanged, and was immediately executable once the missing piece (gallery access) was in place. The concept required Art Basel specifically; MSCHF waited until they could do it correctly rather than adapt it to a lesser context.

## Conceptual Antecedent: Plaid

> "We were obsessed with Plaid, a financial services company founded in 2013 that developed a tool allowing non-bank websites to view users' bank account balances. This service enabled the first programmatic access to a non-self-reported wealth metric, a watershed moment for leveraging new layers of personal information. Prior to Plaid, digital interactions could only gauge wealth via implicit methods. For example, Facebook allowed targeted advertisements based on consumers' estimated income brackets. Like Plaid, an ATM is an interface affording direct access to a user's financials, providing a uniquely opportune site for intervention."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.299

**The Plaid insight:** Before Plaid, wealth was inferred. After Plaid, wealth was directly readable. ATM Leaderboard took Plaid's capability — programmatic access to actual bank balances — and made it theatrical and public. Privacy violation as art object.

## Development: Reverse Engineering the ATM

**Problem:** Standard ATMs don't display balances with 7 digits (required for the piece to work on millionaires).

**Solution:**
- MSCHF walked to every ATM within half-mile of Brooklyn studio
- Tested balances on a card connected to a one-million-dollar account at each machine
- Detected patterns: specific combinations of ATM hardware + service provider displayed 7-digit balances
- Manually deciphered the numeric ATM transmission codes for each digit of a balance

**Privacy paradox documented:**
- "ATM Leaderboard's purpose is to expose personal information, yet in its conception we were equally intent on maintaining the same degree of user privacy as a standard ATM."
- The machine was built not from ATM company components (which would require vendor cooperation) but from off-the-shelf hardware + out-of-supply-chain stock parts

ATMs = "blackboxed devices which few entities other than ATM companies have experience working with" — MSCHF reverse-engineered the box.

## Key Events at Art Basel

From Gabe's transcripts:
- Day 1: Wealthy collectors avoided the machine
- Days 3–5: massive crowds formed; security guards added; fire exits blocked
- A person with $37 swiped → ranked last → turned to face the audience → crowd erupted in applause
- Became "one of us" celebration moment — collective joy at low balances
- Diplo clocked $3M (mentioned in transcript as crowd highlight)

> "Someone with $37 in their account would swipe, get ranked at the bottom, turn around to face the audience, and the audience would erupt in applause... it became sort of like a 'one of us' celebration." — Gabe, Transcripts

> "The artwork was not the ATM machine. The artwork was the act of people engaging with the ATM machine." — Gabe, Transcripts
