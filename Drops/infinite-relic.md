## One-Line Concept

## The Object

A faithful reproduction of a Catholic **monstrance** — the gold sunburst reliquary used to display a consecrated host or sacred relic during adoration. Standard religious iconography: radiating rays, angels, ornate base.

The center compartment, which normally holds the Eucharist or a fragment of a saint's remains, contains a **clinical thermometer** filled with the saint's blood. The thermometer reads room temperature.

Sacred infrastructure → secular measurement. The most intimate possible religious object turned into a barometer for the mundane.

## The Exploit

**Historical problem:** John Calvin identified it in 1534.

In his *Treatise on Relics*, Calvin argued that the Catholic relic market was impossible to reconcile with itself — there were so many claimed relics that "in order to produce the quantity pushed on the public, it would have been necessary for every apostle to have more than four bodies."

**Modern church constraint:** Canon law prohibits *dividing* relics into smaller pieces (to prevent infinite proliferation via subdivision).

**MSCHF's solution:** Don't divide. **Multiply.**

> "Infinite Relic achieved the multiplication of a relic, not its division."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.338

Growing blood cells in vitro from one authentic relic creates unlimited new blood — without ever cutting the original. Theologically, is lab-grown saint's blood a relic? The church's framework has no answer.

## Sourcing the Blood

**Research:** MSCHF studied the relic economy extensively before acquisition:
- "The Russian Store" (Антикварная Лавка & Catholic Relics) — online relic marketplace
- Fluvminales.com — Catholic relic retail platform
- Document reviewed: "Relics in the Church: Authenticity and Preservation" (Vatican, 2017)
- MSCHF described as "one of the largest platforms for the sacred relic market" (via their project website)

**Relic classification system:**
| Class | Type | Examples |
|-------|------|---------|
| Class I | Direct body part | Blood, bone fragment, hair |
| Class II | Object touched by saint | Clothing, personal item |
| Class III | Reproduction | Touched to a relic |

MSCHF needed **Class I** — actual biological material from a canonized saint.

**The acquisition:** Blood from a missionary martyred in **1853** in extreme cold conditions (the Andes region). The body was discovered frozen; blood cells remained intact and viable. Incorruptible saints — those whose bodies resist natural decomposition — are a documented phenomenon in Catholic theology. The perfectly preserved body of Santa Zita (Basilica di San Frediano, Lucca, Italy) is one famous example.

**Transport:** Cold-chained from Italy to Brooklyn using food transport logistics — deliberately avoiding classification as a biological material to bypass FDA oversight.

## The Biology

> "All human blood contains stem cells. MSCHF took the saint's blood, converted the stem cells into the blood cell types needed, cultured them in vitro, and grew red blood cells at scale."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.341

**Output:** ~47 liters of saint-derived blood total across the 500-relic edition.

**FDA navigation:** The project occupied a legal gray zone. Blood sold commercially requires FDA licensing — but MSCHF was not selling blood as a medical product. The relics were distributed as art/religious objects. No FDA classification applied.

> "We initially planned to produce only 500 relics, and as such we were not making false claims or encouraging human consumption of the blood."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.345

## Historical Framework: Calvin's Treatise

**John Calvin, *Treatise on Relics* (1534):**
- Written during the Protestant Reformation as a critique of Catholic relic veneration
- Argued that the sheer volume of claimed relics made the system internally contradictory
- "For every apostle to have the number of relics claimed, each would have needed more than four bodies"
- Protestant Reformation rejected relic veneration entirely
- Modern Catholic guidelines: relics must not be divided; their authenticity must be documented by the bishop who oversaw their preservation

MSCHF found the loophole Calvin didn't imagine: biological multiplication via in vitro culture. The critique stands — the market is oversaturated — but the mechanism is new. Science provided the exploit Calvin's theology couldn't.

- Acquire and transport human biological material across international borders
- Set up in vitro stem cell culture and red blood cell growth
