# Key4All

**Archive #:** 118
**Year:** 2022
**Format:** Experiential / participatory / social sculpture
**Source:** Transcripts (multiple) + *Made by MSCHF* Book p.128–148 (full Absurd Systems documentation)
**Confidence:** HIGH

---

## One-Line Concept

5,000 working keys to one shared car, abandoned under the BQE — owned by the crowd.

---

## Full Description

MSCHF bought a 2004 Chrysler PT Cruiser with wood paneling. They duplicated the car's RFID key frequency and produced 5,000 functional key fobs, each of which could unlock and start the car. They sold keys to people in all 50 states, then abandoned the car under the Brooklyn-Queens Expressway. From that moment, the car belonged to whoever had a key and could find it.

> "Basically what we did is we rebuilt Zipcar if they only had one car."
> — Kevin, *_19 Mar 2024 BUSINESS OF HYPE*

---

## Origin Story: The Apartment Plan That Became a Car

**The original concept was apartment keys — not a car.**

> "We planned to make one thousand keys to a Manhattan apartment block, allowing anyone to claim a key and search for the car from a central location. However, imagining the scenario in which a thousand apartment hunters all followed their instincts simultaneously quickly led us to discard this idea. We wanted to avoid any mishaps, especially our participants being mistaken for intruders. Therefore we raised the number to five thousand and switched to a car."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.130

**Why the pivot mattered:**
> "Pivoting from an apartment to a car shifted the focus from private use to communalism. An apartment is static, requiring users to travel to a fixed location, which would have reduced interaction to a competitive game. A car could travel to keyholders — giving it mobility also allowed the object of desire to become a shared vehicle rather than a shared destination."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.131

## Infrastructure

- **Mobile app:** Key4All app showed real-time car location, keyholder status, "FIND THE CAR" button
- **GPS tracking:** Multiple AirTags embedded in car; public phone number reported location and speed
- **Discord server:** Central community coordination hub — new developments, conflict resolution, regional chapters
- **Key fob:** RFID-enabled; functional indicator LED; GPS coordinates accurate within 150 meters

---

## Launch

**Launch partner:** Casey Neistat (YouTuber). MSCHF loaned him the car to film a demonstration video.

**Day one:** A passerby pulled off the street within hours of launch and drove the car to the Bronx — with Neistat's personal cell phone still inside. MSCHF scrapped their launch plan and chased the car via tracking system.

---

## Key Events in the Car's 9-Month Life

### Philadelphia Jailbreak
A group from Philadelphia found local law enforcement willing to vouch for them. They drove the car to Philadelphia, covered the exterior in stickers bearing their branding, and attempted to prevent other keyholders from locating it via GPS.

The Discord community organized a counter-operation. Keyholders collectively cyberbullied the Philly group into relinquishing the car. The car was liberated and driven to the Philadelphia Zoo, where a keyholder took a triumphant photo. **This was the first documented proof that crowd governance could overcome individual monopolization.**

### Midwest Matcha Chapter
LA-based TikToker @brookejoleneverything drove the car cross-country (Chicago → Colorado → Utah → Nevada → Arizona → California), claiming she was "unwittingly driving a tracked vehicle." She was cyberbullied by the community and suspended from Key4All Discord.

### West Coast and Death
Car traced to Tucson, AZ then California. License plate removed. Car eventually driven into a ditch in Truckee, CA. Engine no longer functional. GPS stopped responding. MSCHF declared the project complete.

### Final State
PT Cruiser retrieved, placed in MSCHF's Perrotin Los Angeles gallery show (*Act 2*, 2024) as a permanent sculpture — completely covered in layers of community-applied paint, drawings, and writing accumulated during its 9-month public life.

> "At the time of writing, the PT Cruiser is available for sale through the Perrotin gallery."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.144

## Legal Model

MSCHF used Zipcar's terms of service as the legal framework. Each keyholder's use operated under terms making them individually responsible for safe driving. MSCHF maintained a formal entity ("Toll-4-All") to handle traffic violations. Real insurance claims were paid out.

## Community Self-Organization

The Key4All community self-organized into:
- Regional Discord chapters (Midwest, West Coast, etc.)
- GoFundMe campaigns for car maintenance ($845+ raised)
- TikTok creator subculture dedicated to tracking/interacting with the car
- Fan repair crews who physically fixed the car (headlights, gas tank, repainting)
- Legal coordination channels

> "You know one thing that strikes me is how many people have donated their own time, service, money and just all-in this project. This has turned into so much more than just another drop."
> — Community TikTok video, cited in *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.137

## Reflections (MSCHF's Own)

> "The success, validity, and execution of Key4All were primarily determined by the MSCHF fan base, which united over the course of nine months to maintain the vehicle, keeping it on the road. Drivers could have approached the car as a dilapidated clunker, to be treated carelessly (and some surely did). However, we continuously observed the opposite: fans cared deeply for the car, sharing and preserving this communal resource."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.145

> "Purchasers of the key had flown in from all over the country, not just to see the thing that they had touched and interacted with show up in a gallery, but they were actually there to meet each other for the first time."
> — Gabe, *TED TALK*

> "This project was never about the car. It was never about the keys. It was about the people."
> — Gabe, *TED TALK*
