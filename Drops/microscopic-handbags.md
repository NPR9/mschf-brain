# Microscopic Handbags

**Archive #:** 157
**Year:** 2023
**Format:** Microscale sculpture / product edition
**Source:** *Made by MSCHF* Book p.322–334 (Absurd Objects section) + Transcripts
**Confidence:** HIGH

---

## One-Line Concept

Luxury handbags physically smaller than a grain of sand — made using biomedical 3D printing, packaged in semiconductor chip carriers, requiring a microscope to see. Sold for $63,750 at Perrotin auction.

---

## The Scale Inversion

MSCHF inverted the luxury handbag scale problem in the most extreme possible direction. Every fashion brand had tried exaggerated proportions — oversized bags, undersized bags. MSCHF went to the physical limit: invisible to the naked eye.

> "There is a platonic ideal [luxury handbag] in the general public's head that is shaped by all of these representations."

The Microscopic Handbag is fully formed — it has handles, stitching detail, hardware, brand insignia — but exists at a scale where none of that is perceptible without equipment. Ownership of a luxury object without access to the luxury experience.

---

## Production: Two-Photon Polymerization

**Technology:** Two-photon polymerization (TPG) — a laser-based 3D printing medium primarily used in biomedical applications (nanomedicine, scaffold structures for tissue engineering).

**Scale:** Each bag smaller than a grain of sand. Physically distinguishable under a low-magnification microscope; invisible to the naked eye.

**Material constraints:**
- Initial resin: translucent neon green (one of few materials available in the biomedical process)
- Process required sculpting technique to accentuate detailing on a transparent surface
- Bag handles needed to be folded or reconfigured for structural durability at microscale
- First batch fell out of its mold in transit — team squinted into microscope for hours before realizing the gel case was empty

**At scale:**
- Printed and packaged in bulk
- "Looked like trays of Himalayan salt"

**Brands reproduced:**
- Hermès (Birkin)
- Jacquemus (Le Chiquito)
- Gucci (Marmont)
- Louis Vuitton
- Telfar

## Packaging

Each bag shipped in a **gel-pak** — the semiconductor industry's standard carrier for transporting integrated circuits and microchips. Rigid plastic tray, blue gel interior, clear top.

Label on each:
```
[BRAND NAME]
MICROSCOPIC HANDBAG
MSCHF
THIS SIDE UP
USE MICROSCOPE
```

The packaging IS the statement: a luxury handbag shipped in the same container as a microchip. The value system of fashion collides with the precision infrastructure of semiconductor logistics.

---

## Two Versions

**Version 1 — Product Edition:**
- Four different handbags, each in its own gel case
- Came with a handheld microscope and instructional scope
- Complex folded multi-layer packaging (styled as a science kit)
- Available for direct purchase

**Version 2 — Sculpture Edition:**
- Each bag mounted on a full laboratory microscope
- Monitor attached to microscope displays magnified image of the bag at 400× scale
- Three microscope stands on pedestals in gallery setting
- Exhibition: *Nothing Is Sacred*, Daelim Museum, Seoul, 2023

## Legal Framework: Hermès v. Rothschild

MSCHF closely watched **Hermès International v. Mason Rothschild** — the NFT Birkin bag case where Hermès sued artist Mason Rothschild for creating digital "MetaBirkins."

The case established the outer limits of luxury brand IP protection against artistic appropriation.

> "We maintain that such appropriation would fall squarely within our First Amendment rights in the form of parody."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.330

MSCHF's legal analysis: Microscopic Handbags qualify as parody under First Amendment protections — the radical scale reduction and explicit labeling as MSCHF products distinguished them from counterfeit goods. They were not selling objects that anyone would mistake for real luxury handbags.

Hermès ultimately prevailed against Rothschild. MSCHF filed Microscopic Handbags with full awareness of that precedent as their legal ceiling.

## The Auction

Sold at Perrotin gallery auction: **$63,750**

Transcript quote from Gabe: "We made a microscopic handbag that sold at auction for $63,000 at Paris Fashion Week at Perel's... making it the world's most expensive handbag per volume. That idea came from a summer intern."

The object that sold for $63,750 is physically invisible without specialized equipment. The price-per-unit-volume calculation makes it the most expensive handbag ever made by that metric — by an order of magnitude.

---

## The Scanning Electron Microscope Images

Documented in book via **scanning electron microscope (SEM)** imagery at 1.0KV × 400 SE(M) magnification, 100μm scale marker:
- Jacquemus Le Chiquito: fully detailed, handles intact, brand proportions recognizable
- Gucci Marmont: GG hardware visible, quilting pattern rendered at nanoscale

These images — the only way to actually *see* the product — became the primary media artifact of the drop.

## Scale as Force Multiplier (Inverted)

MSCHF's [[Scale as Force Multiplier]] principle operates by making things bigger to amplify impact. Microscopic Handbags inverts the direction: going as small as physically possible creates the same amplification effect through paradox.

The object's power scales inversely with its size. A $350 shoe needs to be photographed well to go viral. A nanoscale Birkin is inherently viral because it shouldn't exist.
