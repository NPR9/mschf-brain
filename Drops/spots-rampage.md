# Spot's Rampage

**Archive #:** 58 (Boston Dynamics paintball drop) / 48 (Roblox virtual version — SEPARATE DROP)
**Year:** 2021
**Format:** Remote-controlled robot / interactive performance
**Source:** *Made by MSCHF* Book p.311–321 (Absurd Objects section) + Transcripts

## One-Line Concept

Bought a Boston Dynamics Spot robot for $74,500. Mounted a paintball gun. Let the internet pilot it live for an entire day. Boston Dynamics condemned the drop before it even launched — then NYPD deployed real Spots in the Bronx the same week.

## Full Description

MSCHF purchased a Boston Dynamics Spot robot at full retail price ($74,500) during the invite-only purchase window when Spot first became available to the public. They mounted a paintball gun on the robot and set up a white-walled gallery space with art objects (Venus de Milo replica, Rampage-branded monitors).

On **February 24, 2021**, MSCHF livestreamed full remote public piloting of Spot for the entire day — the **first and perhaps only public remote piloting of Spot** in the world.

Control transferred to a random livestream viewer every two minutes. The gallery floor became increasingly coated in paint; Spot kept falling in the latter half of the day as surfaces became slick.

---

## Genesis: From Fascination to Critique

> "The majority of MSCHF projects start from an initial concept rather than a fully fledged plan. For Spot's Rampage, we had the chance to get a Spot when the robot first became available via invite-only purchase."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.313

- Initially: purchased Spot as "an approximately safe purchase" — fascination with the machine
- Years of Boston Dynamics propaganda: their YouTube videos showed Spot doing choreographed dances; "Do You Love Me" had 40.6M views
- MSCHF became "less infatuated" and more critical: Spot was a DARPA-funded machine with anthropomorphizing marketing concealing military applications
- Multiple options for what the public would do with control; settled on paintball gun

---

## Technical Setup

**Control system:**
- Final control scheme: streamlined, intuitive for anyone who had played a first-person video game
- Paintball gun trigger: manually tested to prevent users from depleting the magazine too quickly

**Camera feeds (two simultaneous):**
1. **Public livestream view** — fixed gallery camera, main broadcast to all viewers
2. **First-person view from Spot's front camera** — secondary interface, visible only to the individual driver for their 2-minute window

**Gallery reality:** As the day progressed, floor became coated in greasy paint → Spot increasingly fell → second half of stream was largely Spot tumbling and unable to traverse the slick surfaces

## The Streisand Effect — Engineered

MSCHF held press previews before launch. Short notice reached Boston Dynamics.

Before Spot's Rampage went live, Boston Dynamics posted a preemptive condemnation on Twitter:

> "Today we learned that an art group is planning a spectacle to draw attention to a provocative use of our industrial robot, Spot®. To be clear, we condemn the portrayal of our technology in any way that promotes violence, harm, or intimidation. Our mission is to create and deliver surprisingly capable robots that inspire delight, and positively impact society... Provocative art can help push useful dialogue about the role of technology in our daily lives. This art, however, fundamentally misrepresents Spot® and how it is being used to benefit our daily lives."
> — Boston Dynamics, Twitter, pre-launch

> "To our delight, they had responded to something that hadn't happened yet, providing the best possible publicity."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.317

Boston Dynamics' preemptive statement became the biggest press beat of the drop — Streisand Effect fully realized. The condemnation announced the drop to audiences MSCHF hadn't reached.

---

## The NYPD Confirmation

The week of Spot's Rampage, NYPD deployed actual Spot robots alongside patrols in the Bronx as part of a pilot program with Boston Dynamics.

Widely circulated surveillance footage of real police Spots appeared simultaneously with MSCHF's paintball Spot.

> "This demonstrated the exact militarization the brand had renounced in Boston Dynamics' Twitter statement just days prior."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.317

MSCHF's critique was not speculative — the actual weaponization was happening in real time. The NYPD footage made Boston Dynamics' condemnation of Spot's Rampage read as pure PR hypocrisy.

---

## The Propaganda War

Boston Dynamics spent years in a propaganda war about Spot:
- Their primary strategy: viral videos of Spot dancing (humanizing, endearing)
- "Do You Love Me" — 40.6M YouTube views; the defining public image of Spot pre-Rampage
- Every few years: a different manufacturer's robot dog with a DJI-mounted gun would go viral → BD's PR would scramble to counter the weaponization narrative
- "It's a dog with a cute name! It can dance!"

After Spot's Rampage: other manufacturers produced similar machines; combat robot imagery became mainstream. Ghost Robotics' DJI-mounted gun robot videos circulated widely. MSCHF's paintball Spot was "one of the few visual narratives that could compete in popularity with the company's glossy promo videos."

---

## Backdoor Termination

MSCHF suspected Boston Dynamics retained backdoor access to Spot. Precaution taken:
- Isolated Spot to a local network before the drop
- Relayed all driving commands to the robot via a separate machine

Despite network isolation, **months after Spot's Rampage**, Boston Dynamics pushed a software update that changed MSCHF's user license expiration date to 1969 — permanently disabling the robot.

> "They did not threaten to disable the robot. Perhaps they were wary of publicizing their ability to remotely destroy a $74,500 product without providing a refund. Alternatively, this method of deactivation may have been unplanned."
> — *Made by MSCHF*, Book p.320

This deactivation proved a second argument MSCHF hadn't explicitly made: **consumers who buy "smart" products don't own them.** The manufacturer retains kill-switch authority. The $74,500 purchase was a revocable license.

---

## Spot's Revenge (Archive #120 context)

The disabled Spot sat in MSCHF's studio as floor decoration for over a year.

For MSCHF's first solo gallery show — *No More Tears, I'm Lovin' It* (Perrotin New York, 2022, Archive #120):
- Spot was resurrected as raw material
- Decorated with a sculptural Gundam-style assemblage of guns rendered in resin — "we decided to pair it with as many guns as possible"
- The disabled robot, unable to move, covered in the weapons it could no longer fire
- Title: "Spot's Revenge"

The disabled Spot became more powerful as sculpture than it had been as an active machine. Boston Dynamics' corporate act of killing the robot made it a more legible art object.
